CORRECTION A — Chapter 7 — Feynman quote and attribution
Priority: Medium
Status: [ ]

Current text paraphrases Feynman's animal argument and
incorrectly attributes the broader thermodynamics point
to Feynman specifically.

Required changes:
1. Replace paraphrase with exact Feynman quote:
   "There is no such thing as polywater because if there
   were, there would also be an animal which didn't need
   to eat food. It would just drink water and excrete
   polywater."
2. Attribute the thermodynamics point (Earth's oceans
   should already be polywater) as a general scientific
   observation — NOT specifically Feynman's.
3. Frame Feynman's contribution as a spoken remark,
   not a published paper.

Replacement paragraph:
"The broader scientific community noted a thermodynamic
problem with polywater from early on. If it were genuinely
more stable than ordinary water, all of Earth's water
should have converted to it long ago. The oceans should
be solid. Life should be impossible.

Feynman put it more elegantly. 'There is no such thing
as polywater,' he said, 'because if there were, there
would also be an animal which didn't need to eat food.
It would just drink water and excrete polywater.' No
such animal existed. It was a quiet devastating
observation from someone who never published a word
on the subject."

Source confirmed: Wikipedia Polywater article, citing
Bauer (note 15). Quote circulates widely across
multiple reliable sources.

CORRECTION B — Chapters 1 and 2 — Tree suction claim
Priority: High
Status: [ ]

Current text implies nobody has fully explained how
trees move water against gravity. This is misleading.

Required changes:
1. Chapter 1 — revise to acknowledge Cohesion-Tension
   theory as the established mainstream explanation.
2. Chapter 2 — same revision.
3. Frame exclusion zone as a possible additional
   mechanism being researched, not a gap-filler for
   an unexplained mystery.

Correct framing:
"The mainstream explanation — Cohesion-Tension theory,
proposed by Dixon and Joly in 1894 — describes how
transpiration pull and the cohesive properties of water
work together to move water upward through xylem vessels.
It is widely accepted and well supported by data. What
the exclusion zone adds — or may add — to that picture
is an active area of research."

Source: Dixon and Joly (1894). Confirmed via multiple
sources including Angeles et al. (2004) letter in
New Phytologist signed by 45 scientists from seven
countries stating CT theory is "the only theory
consistent with the preponderance of data on water
transport in plants."

NOTE: Chapter 8 (The Engine in the Tree) must handle
this carefully and fully when drafted.

CORRECTION D — Chapter 7 — 500 papers vs 500 publications
Priority: Low
Status: [ ]

Current text says "over 500 papers" throughout Chapter 7.

Correct figure: Over 500 total publications — including
peer reviewed papers, popular articles, comments, and
reviews. Not all were peer reviewed scientific papers.

Source: From 1962 to 1974 in the US alone there were
115 research publications and 112 popular articles,
comments and reviews, and 285 polywater-related
publications in other countries. Total approximately 512.

Required change:
Replace "papers" with "publications" wherever the 500
figure is cited in Chapter 7.
